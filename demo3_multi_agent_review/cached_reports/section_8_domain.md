## MAJOR

### M1. Smith vs. Krugman framing overstates the contrast (lines 99–103)
- **Claim:** "Market integration produced concentration of production toward varieties with broad demand, just as Krugman (1979, 1980) … predict, rather than the Smithian specialisation a naive reading of the *Wealth of Nations* would imply."
- **Problem:** §4 (lines 147–150) already concedes that "Stigler (1951)'s own formalisation of the extent-of-the-market doctrine in fact leaves room for increasing returns alongside finer specialisation, so what we test here is the finer-specialisation prediction specifically rather than Smithian thought as a whole." Section 8's prose drops that qualifier and reverts to a clean Smith-vs-Krugman binary. Also, *Krugman (1979, 1980)* predict the home-market effect (more varieties in larger markets) — they do **not** predict mainstream-centroid convergence per se. The distinctiveness result is closer to a NEG / spatial-differentiation prediction (A3–A5 in §4) than a pure Krugman one.
- **Action:** Re-insert §4's Stigler hedge ("the finer-specialisation reading of Smith") and attribute mainstream-centroid convergence to the spatial-differentiation augmentation (Hotelling/Salop/Anderson per §4 line 74) rather than to Krugman alone.

### M2. Per-language vs. pooled story has an unflagged tension for entropy (lines 70–82)
- **Claim:** "Specialisation-entropy rises with log(MA) … pooled 0.751***, positive in all six per-language regressions, significant in two."
- **Problem:** Only 2/6 per-language coefficients are individually significant (Italian, French) and German is essentially zero (0.093). §7 line 105 explicitly states: "Where the pooled and per-language results disagree we discuss the heterogeneity directly rather than relying on the pooled coefficient alone." Section 8 then proceeds to write a strong pooled conclusion ("completes the picture") without that direct discussion for entropy. By contrast, the diversity null (German) and the distinctiveness null (Latin) each get a paragraph of interpretation. Entropy gets none.
- **Action:** Add one or two sentences acknowledging that the entropy pooled effect is driven primarily by Italian and French, and explain why the remaining vernaculars and Latin are underpowered (likely fewer titles × topic cells, since entropy over 10 clusters needs more support than a centroid-distance metric).

## MINOR

### m1. "Standard-deviation movement" magnitude claim is loose (lines 30–33)
- **Claim:** "a 1-log-unit increase in MA (about a 2–2.5 within-city standard-deviation movement) is associated with roughly a 0.47·SD_y increase in diversity"
- **Problem:** This conflates an unstandardised coefficient (0.467) with a standardised one. 0.467 is the slope in raw diversity units per log MA unit. The "0.47·SD_y" reading is correct only if SD(diversity) ≈ 1 in the regression sample, which should be verified, not assumed.
- **Action:** Either drop the SD-of-y phrasing or state the within-city SD of the diversity metric explicitly.

### m2. Robustness count "24 of 30" needs unpacking (line 108)

- **Problem:** 6 (metric, language) pairs flip sign unweighted. That is 20% of the per-language cells. The text presents this as benign, but does not say which 6 flipped.
- **Action:** Add a parenthetical identifying which pairs flipped, or point to a robustness table that does.

### m3. Conditional-prediction logic underplayed for Latin distinctiveness null (lines 56–63)
- **Claim:** Latin null on distinctiveness is interpreted as "Latin readership was pan-European … cannot pull the city's centroid closer to the European centroid than it already is."
- **Problem:** Plausible economic intuition, but it does not map cleanly to the A1–A5 framework of §4. Per §4 lines 132–134, a near-zero distinctiveness slope "points to A3 or A4 failing." Latin's pan-European character is exactly an A3 failure — but the text does not make that mapping explicit.
- **Action:** One sentence noting that the Latin null is the predicted footprint of A3 failing for Latin specifically.

### m4. Permutation diagnostic interpretation (lines 142–151)
- **Claim:** "The point estimate under this diagnostic is smaller than the headline of 0.467, consistent with part of the headline magnitude reflecting a mechanical vocabulary-coverage contribution."
- **Problem:** The permutation 95% interval [0.124, 0.165] is roughly a third of the headline 0.467. That is not "part of" — that is most of the magnitude being mechanical. The text correctly says the sign-based prediction survives, but "part of the headline magnitude" undersells how much shrinks.
- **Action:** State the proportionate shrinkage more frankly, or note that the framework predicts only the sign, not the magnitude.

### m5. Cross-spec correlations 0.81–0.97: which is the floor? (lines 109–113)
- **Claim:** "cross-spec correlations on log(MA) of 0.81–0.97 (with the headline Bairoch spec correlating ≥ 0.91 with each of the other three)."
- **Problem:** If Bairoch correlates ≥ 0.91 with each of the other three, then the 0.81 floor must be a between-non-headline-spec correlation. Reader naturally reads "0.81" as a worst-case for the headline spec, which it is not.
- **Action:** Clarify that 0.81 is the floor across the (4 choose 2) = 6 pairs and that the Bairoch-anchored floor is 0.91.

### m6. Two-way clustering: "Point estimates are unchanged by construction" needs a guard (lines 156–158)
- **Problem:** Reads as if this is a feature of the Cameron-Miller two-way procedure specifically. It is a feature of any clustered-SE re-weighting that does not change the moment conditions.
- **Action:** Either delete "by construction" or replace with "since clustering affects only the variance estimator."
